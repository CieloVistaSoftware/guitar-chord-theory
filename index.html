What is a chord?

A chord needs at least 3 notes. A major chord is spelled 1 – 3 – 5 (root, major 3rd, perfect 5th).

Look at the fretboard below -- that's a C major chord, in open position. The low E string is muted, so it's not part of the shape at all. From there: the A string, 3rd fret is marked 1 in red -- that's the root, C. The D string, 2nd fret is marked 3 in yellow -- the major 3rd, E. The G string rings open, shown as a plain O -- that's the 5th, G. The B string, 1st fret circles back to 1 -- the root, C, again. And the high E string rings open too, another plain O -- another 3rd, E. Five strings, five notes, but only three different letters -- C, E, and G -- each one just repeated so there's a note ringing on every string you strum, not only three.

The 7 modes of the major scale

A mode isn't a different set of notes -- it's the exact same major scale, just starting (and resolving) on a different one of its own 7 degrees instead of the 1st. C major starting on its 2nd degree (D) is D Dorian; starting on its 6th degree (A) is A Aeolian -- same notes, different "home."

Pick a mode below and it plays that same scale, one octave, starting and ending on that mode's own tonic -- listen for how the character changes even though every note still belongs to the current key.
